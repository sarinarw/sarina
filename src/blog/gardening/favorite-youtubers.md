Most of what I've learned about gardening came from YouTube before it came from the garden. Here are the three channels I keep coming back to.

## Millenial Gardener

The channel that got me actually doing things instead of just watching. The pacing is unhurried — you get the reasoning behind decisions, not just the steps. There's a lot of focus on growing food in the real constraints most people have: limited space, limited time, not a lot of prior knowledge.

What I appreciate most is the seasonal honesty. Things die, experiments fail, and that gets covered too. It makes the wins feel credible.

## Edge of Nowhere Farm

More homestead-scale than the others, which I find useful even though I'm not anywhere near that kind of operation. Watching someone manage a larger growing system gives you a different lens on the small-scale stuff — you start thinking about soil health, seed saving, and perennials differently when you see how they fit into a longer-term picture.

The production style is very unpolished in a good way. It feels like following someone who's actually farming, not performing farming.

## Epic Gardening

Kevin Espiritu's channel is the most produced of the three and also the broadest. If I want to know something specific — what's wrong with my tomatoes, how to set up a drip system, whether grow bags or raised beds make more sense for a particular situation — this is usually my first stop. The back catalog is enormous and well-organized.

The channel has grown a lot and gets more commercial over time, but the practical information holds up. The [Epic Homesteading](https://www.youtube.com/@EpicHomesteading) spin-off is worth watching too if you're interested in the larger-scale stuff.

---

If you're just getting started, I'd say watch Millenial Gardener for the mindset, Edge of Nowhere Farm for the long view, and Epic Gardening when you have a specific problem to solve. That's roughly the order I encountered them in, and it worked out.
